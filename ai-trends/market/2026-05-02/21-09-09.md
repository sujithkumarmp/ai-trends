Based on the constraints of defining a high-probability, defined-risk strategy with an available structure, the optimal approach is to employ an **Iron Condor**.

An Iron Condor is a neutral strategy designed to profit when the underlying asset's price remains within a specified range until expiration. It involves selling premium on both the call side and the put side, thereby collecting a net credit upfront.

***

### 📈 Defined Strategy: Short Iron Condor (Range-Bound Trade)

**Objective:** Profit from consolidation or sideways movement.
**Hypothesis:** The asset is currently in a consolidation phase between a defined support level ($\text{Put Exit}$) and a defined resistance level ($\text{Call Exit}$).
**Risk Profile:** Defined and limited to the width of the spread.
**Reward Profile:** Defined and limited to the net credit received.

#### 📊 Simulated Trade Entry (Hypothetical Values):

To execute this trade, we assume the current price is in a consolidation zone allowing the profitable establishment of the wings.

| Leg | Action | Strike Price | Premium Paid/Received | Role |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | Sell Put ($\text{Short Put}$) | **190** | $+\$0.40$ (Credit) | Defines lower boundary of profit. |
| **2** | Buy Put ($\text{Long Put}$) | **185** | $-\$0.15$ (Debit) | Defines maximum risk buffer below. |
| **3** | Sell Call ($\text{Short Call}$) | **215** | $+\$0.35$ (Credit) | Defines upper boundary of profit. |
| **4** | Buy Call ($\text{Long Call}$) | **220** | $-\$0.20$ (Debit) | Defines maximum risk buffer above. |

**Net Premium Received (Credit):** $\$0.40 + \$0.35 - \$0.15 - \$0.20 = \mathbf{\$0.40}$ per share (or $\$40$ per contract).

***

### 🛡️ Trade Analysis and Risk/Reward Geometry

#### 1. Profit Zone (Profit Target)
The sweet spot for this trade is any price movement that keeps the asset between the two sold strikes:
*   **Zone of Maximum Profit:** Anywhere between **190 and 215**.
*   **Maximum Profit Potential:** $\text{Net Credit Received} = \mathbf{\$0.40}$

#### 2. Break-Even Points (BEP)
If the price moves significantly, the trade will lose money, but the loss is capped.
*   **Upper Break-Even Point:** $\text{Sell Call Strike} + \text{Net Credit} (215 + \$0.40) = \mathbf{\$215.40}$
*   **Lower Break-Even Point:** $\text{Sell Put Strike} - \text{Net Credit} (190 - \$0.40) = \mathbf{\$189.60}$

#### 3. Maximum Loss (Max Risk)
*   **Max Loss on the Put Side:** If the price drops below $\mathbf{\$185}$. (Max loss: $\text{Strike Difference} - \text{Credit}$)
*   **Max Loss on the Call Side:** If the price rises above $\mathbf{\$220}$. (Max loss: $\text{Strike Difference} - \text{Credit}$)

***

### ✅ Justification and Risk Management Summary

1.  **High Probability Focus:** By selling both the puts and calls, this trade capitalizes on the statistical likelihood that the asset will not make a massive move in either direction over the short term.
2.  **Defined Risk:** The maximum potential loss is strictly limited to the distance between the bought and sold wings. This is critical for portfolio management.
3.  **Optimal Exit Strategy:** This trade should be viewed as a **premium collection play**. If the asset moves favorably and reaches the break-even points quickly, the position can be managed or exited early to capture a portion of the profit $(\text{e.g., selling 50\% of the width remaining})$.
